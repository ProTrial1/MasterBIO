1. Phytohormone Fundamentals

This section outlines the basic definition and primary classes of phytohormones. Understanding these broad categories is required before analyzing their specific biochemical synthesis pathways. Hormones act as chemical messengers coordinating plant growth, development, and responses to stimuli at very low concentrations.

🌱

Auxins (IAA)

Cell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation. Synthesized primarily in shoot apical meristems.

🍀

Gibberellins (GA)

Stem elongation, seed germination, flowering. Synthesized in young tissues and developing seeds.

🍃

Cytokinins (CK)

Cell division, delay of senescence. Synthesized primarily in root tips and transported upwards.

🍂

Abscisic Acid (ABA)

Stress response (drought), stomatal closure, seed dormancy. Synthesized in mature leaves and roots.

🍊

Ethylene

Fruit ripening, senescence, leaf abscission. A gaseous hormone synthesized in aging tissues.

2. Detailed Synthesis & Regulation: Indole-3-Acetic Acid (IAA)

Indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) is the most abundant naturally occurring auxin. Its synthesis is highly regulated. Plants utilize multiple pathways to synthesize IAA, mostly originating from the amino acid Tryptophan (Trp). This section allows you to interactively step through the dominant Tryptophan-dependent pathway (the IPA pathway) and explore how IAA pools are regulated within the cell.

Summary: The most critical auxin biosynthesis pathway involves the conversion of L-Tryptophan to IPA by the TAA family of aminotransferases, followed by the rate-limiting conversion of IPA to IAA by YUCCA family flavin monooxygenases.

Regulation of IAA Homeostasis

Cells must tightly control the amount of active IAA. This is achieved through a combination of synthesis, conjugation, degradation, and transport.

Conjugation 🔒

IAA is reversibly bound to amino acids (e.g., via GH3 enzymes) or sugars. Conjugates are inactive storage forms that can be hydrolyzed back to free IAA when needed.

Degradation 🗑

Irreversible breakdown of active IAA. The main pathway involves oxidation to oxIAA by DAO (Dioxygenase for auxin oxidation) enzymes, preventing toxic auxin accumulation.

Transport (PAT) ⇅

Polar Auxin Transport utilizes influx carriers (AUX1/LAX) and efflux carriers (PIN proteins). Asymmetric PIN localization directs auxin flow, establishing gradients crucial for organogenesis.

3. Synthesis & Regulation of Other Major Hormones

While Auxin is foundational, plant development requires the coordinated action of multiple hormones. This section summarizes the basic synthesis and regulatory logic for Gibberellins, Cytokinins, ABA, and Ethylene. Click the tabs to switch between hormone profiles.

Gibberellin (GA) Pathway

Synthesis

Terpenoid pathway originating in plastids.

GGPP → (CPS/KS) → ent-Kaurene → (KO/KAO) → GA12 → Bioactive GA (GA1, GA4)

Regulation

  • Activation: GA20ox and GA3ox enzymes catalyze final steps to active forms.
  • Deactivation: GA2ox enzymes hydroxylate active GAs to inactive forms (e.g., GA29, GA34).
  • Feedback loop: High GA levels inhibit GA20ox/GA3ox and promote GA2ox.

Cytokinin (CK) Pathway

Synthesis

Derived from adenine and an isoprenoid side chain.

ATP/ADP/AMP + DMAPP → (IPT enzyme) → iPRTP/iPDP/iPMP → Active CKs (Zeatin, iP)

Regulation

  • Degradation: Cytokinin oxidase/dehydrogenase (CKX) irreversibly cleaves the side chain, destroying activity.
  • Conjugation: Reversible conjugation to glucose (O-glucosylation) or irreversible conjugation (N-glucosylation).

Abscisic Acid (ABA) Pathway

Synthesis

Synthesized from carotenoids (C40) in plastids, finished in cytosol.

Zeaxanthin → Violaxanthin → (NCED) → Xanthoxin → ABA

*NCED (9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase) is the key rate-limiting step, highly upregulated by drought stress.

Regulation

  • Catabolism: Hydroxylation via CYP707A enzymes (cytochrome P450s) to phaseic acid (PA), which is inactive.
  • Conjugation: Stored as ABA-glucose ester (ABA-GE) in vacuoles; rapidly hydrolyzed by BG1 (β-glucosidase) under stress.

Ethylene Pathway

Synthesis

Derived from the amino acid Methionine (Yang Cycle).

Methionine → SAM → (ACS) → ACC → (ACO) → Ethylene (Gas)

*ACS (ACC synthase) is the main regulatory node. ACO (ACC oxidase) requires oxygen.

Regulation

  • Transcriptional/Post-Translational: ACS enzymes are highly regulated at the protein stability level (phosphorylated/degraded via proteasome).
  • Feedback: Can exhibit autoinhibition (vegetative tissues) or autocatalysis (ripening climacteric fruits).
  • Since it's a gas, it diffuses away easily; synthesis rates dictate internal concentration.
