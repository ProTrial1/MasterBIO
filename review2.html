Introduction to Ecology

The scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment.

Ecology integrates biology, geography, and earth sciences. It is essential for understanding the distribution, abundance, and evolution of life on Earth, as well as managing conservation efforts and predicting the impacts of climate change.

🔬

Autecology

The study of individual organisms or single species in relation to their environment. Focuses heavily on physiology, behavior, and evolutionary adaptations to specific conditions.

🌎

Synecology

The study of groups of organisms of different species (communities) associated together as a unit. Focuses on food webs, energy flow, and complex interspecific interactions.

Population Dynamics

Demographics, dispersion, growth models, and life history strategies.

Population Dispersion

Dispersion describes the spatial relationship between individuals within a population's boundaries.

Clumped

Individuals aggregate in patches. Most common pattern. Driven by uneven resource distribution or social behavior (e.g., herds, schools of fish).

Uniform

Evenly spaced. Driven by antagonistic interactions like territoriality in animals or chemical inhibition (allelopathy) in plants.

Random

Position is independent of others. Occurs when resources are constant across the area and biological interactions are weak (e.g., windblown seeds).

Growth Models

Compare idealized unlimited growth (J-curve) with realistic growth constrained by environmental limits (S-curve).

Survivorship Curves

A plot of the number of individuals alive at each age. Note the logarithmic Y-axis.

Type I Low early death, late decline (Humans).
Type II Constant mortality rate (Birds).
Type III Massive early death, high survival later (Fish).

⚖️ Life History Strategies (r vs K Selection)

r-Selected Species

  • → Maximize reproductive rate (r).
  • → Many small offspring, early maturation.
  • → Little to no parental care.
  • → Type III survivorship curve.
  • → Environment: Unstable, density-independent.
  • Examples: Weeds, insects, bacteria.

K-Selected Species

  • → Hover near carrying capacity (K).
  • → Few, large offspring, late maturation.
  • → High degree of parental care.
  • → Type I survivorship curve.
  • → Environment: Stable, density-dependent.
  • Examples: Elephants, humans, oak trees.

From Populations to Communities: Biodiversity

When multiple populations occupy the same habitat, we measure their combined biodiversity using mathematical indices.

Two Components of Diversity

  • Species Richness (S): The total number of different species present.
  • Species Evenness: The relative abundance of each species. A community is more diverse if populations are evenly distributed rather than dominated by one species.

Key Indices

Shannon Index (H'):
H' = - Σ (pi × ln(pi))

Accounts for both richness and evenness. Higher value = higher diversity (typically 1.5 to 3.5).

Simpson's Index of Diversity (1-D):
1 - D = 1 - Σ (pi2)

Probability that two randomly selected individuals belong to different species. Ranges from 0 (no diversity) to 1 (infinite diversity).

Community Ecology

Niches, symbiosis, trophic cascades, and succession.

The Ecological Niche

A species' niche is its multi-dimensional hypervolume—its specific role and requirements regarding all biotic and abiotic factors in its environment.

Competitive Exclusion Principle

Gause's Law: Two species competing for the exact same limiting resources cannot coexist. One will outcompete the other to local extinction, or evolutionary pressures will force Resource Partitioning (dividing the niche).

Fundamental Niche

The full potential physiological range a species could occupy.

Realized Niche

The actual, narrower space it occupies due to competition & predation.

Ecological Succession

The sequential, predictable change in community composition over time following a disturbance.

Primary Succession

Starts on completely barren rock with no soil (e.g., volcanic island, glacial retreat). Pioneer species like lichens must slowly break down rock to create soil. Takes centuries.

Secondary Succession

Occurs where a disturbance (fire, farming) cleared the community but left soil intact. Recovers much faster, progressing to a climax community.

Interspecific Interactions

Hover over the cards to study symbiotic and antagonistic relationships.

🤝

Mutualism

(+, +)

Both species benefit.

Ex: Zooxanthellae algae in corals. Algae provide sugars via photosynthesis; coral provides a safe, sunlit home.

🐳

Commensalism

(+, 0)

One benefits, one neutral.

Ex: Cattle egrets eating insects flushed out by grazing water buffalo. Buffalo is unaffected.

🦠

Parasitism

(+, -)

Parasite benefits, host harmed.

Ex: Endoparasites (tapeworms) live inside the host; ectoparasites (ticks) feed on the exterior surface.

🦀

Predation &
Herbivory

(+, -)

Consumer benefits, prey dies.

Drives intense evolutionary arms races. Leads to mechanical defenses, chemical toxins, and mimicry.

🐠 Keystone Species & Trophic Cascades

A keystone species exerts strong control on community structure not by numerical might, but by its pivotal ecological role. Removing them causes a trophic cascade—a chain reaction altering multiple levels of the food web.

Classic Example: Sea otters prey on sea urchins. If otters are removed, urchin populations explode and overgraze kelp forests, destroying the entire habitat for many other species.

Ecosystems & Energy

Energy flux, primary production, and biogeochemical cycling.

Primary Production

The amount of light energy converted to chemical energy by autotrophs (plants, algae). This sets the "energy budget" for the entire ecosystem.

GPP (Gross Primary Production) Total light energy assimilated.
- Ra (Autotrophic Respiration) Energy used by producers for their own cellular respiration.
= NPP (Net Primary Production) Energy stored as biomass, available to consumers.

Energy Transfer: 10% Rule

Secondary production is chemical energy in food converted to new consumer biomass. Only ~10% transfers up. Heat is lost at every step.

This massive energy loss is why top apex predators are rare, and food chains rarely exceed 4 or 5 trophic levels.

Biogeochemical Cycles

Nutrients circulate via biological, geological, and chemical processes. Select a cycle to explore.

C The Carbon Cycle

Carbon forms the framework of all organic molecules. The balance is largely maintained by photosynthesis and respiration.

  • Photosynthesis: Autotrophs remove CO₂ from the atmosphere to build glucose.
  • Cellular Respiration: All organisms break down glucose for ATP, returning CO₂ to the air.
  • Fossil Fuels: Long-term storage (sinks) in the earth. Burning these rapidly releases massive amounts of ancient CO₂, causing global warming.

N The Nitrogen Cycle

Crucial for amino acids (proteins) and nucleic acids. The atmosphere is 78% N₂, but it is unusable to plants directly.

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Specialized bacteria (e.g., in legume roots) convert N₂ gas to ammonia (NH₃/NH₄⁺).
  • Nitrification: Soil bacteria convert ammonium to nitrates (NO₃⁻), the form plants prefer to absorb.
  • Denitrification: Other bacteria convert nitrates back to N₂ gas in anaerobic, waterlogged soil.

H₂O The Water Cycle

Water drives global climate and biome distribution. It is a physical process, unlike the chemical changes in C and N cycles.

  • Evaporation: Solar energy turns liquid water to vapor.
  • Transpiration: Massive amounts of evaporation specifically driven by plant leaves.
  • Precipitation: Condensed vapor falls back to Earth, sustaining terrestrial life.

P The Phosphorus Cycle

Major constituent of nucleic acids, phospholipids, and ATP. Critically: It lacks a significant gas phase in the atmosphere.

  • Weathering: Slow breakdown of rocks adds phosphate (PO₄³⁻) to soil.
  • Leaching & Runoff: Rain washes phosphates into aquatic systems. Excess from human fertilizers causes severe algal blooms (eutrophication).
  • Sedimentation: Phosphates settle on the ocean floor to eventually become new rock over millions of years.

Global Biomes

Major life zones characterized by climate and physical environments.

Terrestrial Biomes

Determined primarily by climate (Mean Temp & Precipitation). The Whittaker plot visualizes these boundaries.

Aquatic Zonation

Aquatic biomes form the largest part of the biosphere. They are physically stratified based on light penetration and depth.

Intertidal Zone
Intertidal: Periodically submerged by tides. Brutal physical environment requiring adaptations against desiccation and wave force.
