Roots: Growth Zones

Roots grow exclusively from the apex, divided into three distinct zones:

1. Zone of Cell Division (RAM)

The apical meristem actively undergoing mitosis. Shielded by the Root Cap, which secretes mucilage to lubricate passage through abrasive soil and contains statoliths for gravitropism.

2. Zone of Elongation

Cells rapidly elongate (driven by auxin and vacuole turgor pressure), physically pushing the root tip deeper into the soil.

3. Zone of Maturation (Differentiation)

Cells complete their differentiation into functional tissues (xylem, phloem, endodermis). Epidermal cells extend out to form Root Hairs for massive surface area absorption.

🎋

Stems: Secondary Growth

While monocots only grow taller, dicots and gymnosperms grow wider via lateral meristems.

The Vascular Cambium

A cylinder of meristematic cells one cell layer thick. It divides to add:

  • Secondary Xylem (Wood): Accumulated towards the inside. Older xylem becomes clogged with resins (heartwood), while newer xylem actively transports water (sapwood). Growth rings represent seasonal xylem deposition.
  • Secondary Phloem (Inner Bark): Accumulated towards the outside. Does not build up massively because older phloem gets crushed and sloughed off as the tree expands.
The Cork Cambium (Phellogen)

Produces thick, waxy cork cells outward to replace the original epidermis, protecting the expanding stem from pathogens and water loss. (Cork + Cork Cambium = Periderm / Outer Bark).

Water & Mineral Transport

Thermodynamic movement of bulk fluids and the active, ATP-driven molecular pumps required to concentrate soil nutrients.

Cohesion-Tension Theory

$\Psi = \Psi_s + \Psi_p$

Water moves passively from higher to lower (more negative) water potential ($\Psi$). Adding solutes ($\Psi_s$) makes potential negative. Physical tension ($\Psi_p$) makes it vastly negative.

Mechanism: Solar evaporation at the leaf creates severe negative pressure. Because water molecules hydrogen-bond to each other (Cohesion) and to the xylem walls (Adhesion), this tension physically pulls the entire fluid column up from the soil.

Root Cortex Pathways

Water and minerals travel from the root hair to the vascular cylinder (stele) through distinct cellular routes before hitting the Endodermis.

Apoplastic Route

Movement entirely through the continuous network of non-living cell walls and extracellular spaces. It is fast and unregulated. Blocked by the Casparian Strip.

Symplastic Route

Movement through the living cytosol, passing from cell to cell via cytoplasmic channels called plasmodesmata. Highly regulated selective uptake.

Transmembrane Route

Water crosses plasma membranes and cell walls repeatedly, heavily relying on specialized membrane water channels called Aquaporins.

Active Mineral Absorption

Soil minerals are highly dilute. Roots must expend massive amounts of cellular energy (ATP) to concentrate essential elements against their gradient.

1. De Novo Synthesis (Plastid)

Plant lipid synthesis is unique; unlike animals, the de novo creation of fatty acids occurs entirely within plastids (chloroplasts in leaves, amyloplasts/leucoplasts in seeds), not the cytosol.

  • ACCase Enzyme: Acetyl-CoA Carboxylase converts Acetyl-CoA (2C) into Malonyl-CoA (3C). This is the critical, highly regulated, rate-limiting step.
  • FAS Complex: Fatty Acid Synthase repeatedly condenses Malonyl-ACP units, adding 2 carbons per cycle (while releasing CO₂). It uses massive amounts of NADPH (from light reactions or pentose phosphate pathway) to reduce the chain.
  • Termination: The cycle stops at 16:0 (Palmitic acid) or 18:0 (Stearic acid). A desaturase in the plastid may introduce one double bond (forming 18:1 Oleic acid) before export.

2. Assembly & Storage (ER)

Free fatty acids are exported to the cytosol, converted to Acyl-CoAs, and transported to the Endoplasmic Reticulum for assembly via the Kennedy Pathway.

  • Structural Lipids: Phosphatidic Acid (PA) is converted into diacylglycerol (DAG) and then modified with various head groups (e.g., choline, ethanolamine) to form the glycerophospholipids that make up all cellular membrane bilayers.
  • Storage Lipids (TAGs): In developing seeds (like Canola, Soy, Peanut), a third fatty acid is attached to DAG to form highly hydrophobic Triacylglycerol (TAG).
  • Oil Bodies: Because TAGs are completely insoluble in water, they "bud off" from the ER membrane, encapsulated by a phospholipid monolayer coated in stabilizing proteins called Oleosins, forming seed oil bodies.

Evolutionary Adaptations to Photorespiration

To survive in harsh climates, certain plants evolved mechanisms to forcefully concentrate CO₂ around RuBisCO, completely eliminating wasteful photorespiration.

C3 Pathway

Cool & Moist Climates

Examples: Rice, Wheat, Soybeans, Trees. CO₂ is fixed directly by RuBisCO in mesophyll cells. Vulnerable to O₂ binding if stomata close.

C4 Pathway (Spatial Separation)

Hot & Sunny Climates

Examples: Corn, Sugarcane. Uses Kranz Anatomy. CO₂ is fixed by PEP Carboxylase (ignores O₂) in Mesophyll, converted to Malate, and pumped deep into the Bundle-Sheath cell to saturate RuBisCO.

CAM Pathway (Temporal Separation)

Extreme Arid/Desert

Examples: Cacti, Pineapples. Stomata open ONLY at night to collect CO₂ (stored as Malic Acid in the vacuole). By day, stomata lock shut, and the acid releases CO₂ for the Calvin Cycle.

Biochemical Pathway Breakdown

Glycolysis (Sugar Splitting)

The 10-step enzymatic pathway in the cytoplasm. It requires 2 ATP initially (Investment Phase) to destabilize Glucose into Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. This 6C molecule splits into two 3C G3P molecules. During the Payoff Phase, substrate-level phosphorylation yields 4 ATP and 2 NADH, ending with 2 molecules of Pyruvate.

The Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

Pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix, is converted to Acetyl CoA, and joins Oxaloacetate to form Citrate. Through decarboxylation and redox reactions, the carbons are oxidized to CO₂. The primary purpose is to harvest high-energy electrons onto NAD⁺ and FAD, forming NADH and FADH₂.

Oxidative Phosphorylation

NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the Electron Transport Chain (Complexes I-IV). The energy drop pumps H⁺ into the intermembrane space. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water. Protons rush back into the matrix via ATP Synthase (chemiosmosis), generating ~28 ATP per glucose.

Anaerobic Respiration (Fermentation)

When oxygen is absent (e.g., flooded roots), the ETC halts. Fermentation acts as an emergency pathway to oxidize NADH back into NAD⁺ so Glycolysis can continue producing 2 ATP. In plants, this typically results in Alcoholic Fermentation (Pyruvate ➔ Ethanol + CO₂), which can be toxic if flooding persists.

Environmental Stress Responses

Detailed molecular cascades and anatomical adaptations plants deploy to survive harsh abiotic stressors.

Growth & Phytohormones

The molecular signal transduction pathways dictating growth, cellular differentiation, and reproductive development.

Auxin (IAA)

Master growth hormone. Responsible for cell elongation via Acid Growth Hypothesis (stimulating H+ pumps to activate expansins), apical dominance (inhibiting lateral buds), and phototropism/gravitropism via asymmetric polar transport.

Signal Transduction: Repressor Degradation

Auxin binds the nuclear TIR1 receptor (an F-box protein). This acts as a molecular glue, recruiting Aux/IAA repressor proteins. The complex polyubiquitinates the repressors, marking them for destruction by the 26S proteasome. This frees ARFs (Auxin Response Factors) to transcribe growth genes.

Gibberellins (GAs)

Crucial for massive stem hyper-elongation (bolting) and seed germination. Embryo-produced GA diffuses to the aleurone layer to stimulate alpha-amylase synthesis, which breaks down endosperm starch into sugars.

Signal Transduction: DELLA Degradation

Bioactive GA binds the soluble GID1 receptor. This complex binds to DELLA proteins (master growth repressors). The DELLAs are subsequently ubiquitinated and destroyed by the proteasome, unleashing transcription factors (like PIFs) to promote growth.

Cytokinins (CK)

Promotes cell division (cytokinesis), delays leaf senescence, and encourages lateral shoot branching. Operates via a Two-Component Phosphorelay system involving AHK receptors and ARR transcription factors.

Ethylene (C₂H₄)

The gaseous hormone dictating fruit ripening, leaf abscission, and the triple response. Operates via negative regulation: Ethylene inactivates its receptor, shutting off the CTR1 kinase repressor, allowing EIN2/EIN3 to turn on ripening genes.

Abscisic Acid (ABA)

The ultimate stress hormone. Triggers rapid stomatal closure during drought by causing guard cell K+ efflux. Maintains seed dormancy. Operates by binding PYR/PYL receptors, which inhibit PP2C phosphatases, freeing SnRK2 kinases to activate stress responses.
