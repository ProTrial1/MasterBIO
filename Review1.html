📊 Module 1: Classical (Transmission) Genetics

This section introduces the foundational rules of inheritance discovered by Gregor Mendel. You will find interactive Punnett squares to visualize offspring probabilities, explore extensions beyond simple dominance, and understand how traits are mapped across generations using pedigrees. Interact with the cross views below to compare genetic ratios.

Mendel's Three Laws

  • 1. Law of Dominance: In a heterozygote, the dominant allele masks the recessive allele.
  • 2. Law of Segregation: Alleles of a locus segregate into separate gametes randomly.
  • 3. Independent Assortment: Alleles of one gene sort independently of another (exception: linked genes).

Probability Rules

Product Rule (AND): Independent events occurring simultaneously (multiply probabilities).

Sum Rule (OR): Mutually exclusive events (add probabilities).

Extensions of Mendelism

Incomplete Dominance

Blending of homozygous phenotypes. Heterozygote is intermediate.

Mirabilis Flowers:
CRCR (Red) × CWCW (White)
→ 100% CRCW (Pink)
Codominance

Simultaneous expression of two alleles. No blending.

  • Cattle Coat: Red × White → Roan (both hairs present)
  • Horse Coat: Chestnut × Cremello → Palomino
Multiple Alleles

More than 2 allelic variations for a single gene.

ABO Blood Group:
Determined by antigens.
IA and IB are codominant.
Both IA and IB are dominant over i.
Epistasis

One gene masks the expression of another (hypostatic) gene.

  • Recessive (9:3:4): Mice coat color (Black/Brown/Albino)
  • Dominant (12:3:1): Squash fruit color (White/Yellow/Green)

🔬 Module 2: The Chromosomal Basis

This module transitions from abstract alleles to physical chromosomes. Compare the processes of Mitosis and Meiosis using the tabs below to understand how genetic material is conserved in somatic cells versus reduced in gametes. Review how chromosomal linkage and structural abnormalities impact inheritance.

Equational Division

Yields two genetically identical diploid (2n) somatic cells.

2n
→
2n
2n

Focuses on organismal growth and tissue repair. No crossing over occurs.

Reductional Division

Yields four genetically distinct haploid (n) gametes. Essential for sexual reproduction.

Meiosis I
  • Prophase I: Crossing over (homologous recombination) at chiasmata generates diversity.
  • Anaphase I: Homologous chromosomes separate.
Meiosis II
  • Similar to mitosis.
  • Anaphase II: Sister chromatids separate.

Linkage & Mapping Math

Genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together. Recombination Frequency (RF) measures the distance between them.

RF = (Recombinants / Total Offspring) × 100
  • 1% RF = 1 Map Unit / CentiMorgan (cM).
  • Max measurable RF is 50% (appears unlinked).
  • Three-Point Testcross: Identifies double crossovers to determine gene order.

Chromosomal Abnormalities

Aneuploidy vs Polyploidy

Aneuploidy: Abnormal number (e.g., Trisomy 21). Polyploidy: Extra complete sets (3n, 4n).

🧬 Module 3: Molecular Genetics

Explore the chemical basis of heredity. This section contrasts ancestral (prokaryotic) DNA replication with eukaryotic models, visualizes the Central Dogma (flow of information), and outlines the mechanisms of gene expression regulation, including operons and epigenetics.

DNA Structure

Double helix, antiparallel strands (5' → 3' and 3' → 5').

A 2 H-Bonds T
G 3 H-Bonds C

Nucleotides consist of a phosphate, deoxyribose sugar, and a nitrogenous base.

Replication: Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

Ancestral Bacterial (Prokaryotic)
  • Single origin of replication (oriC).
  • Theta (θ) Replication: Bidirectional around circular chromosome.
  • DNA Pol III is main synthesizer.
  • Ends at ter region, resolving catenanes via Topoisomerase IV.
Eukaryotic Contrast
  • Multiple origins of replication.
  • Linear chromosomes.
  • Requires telomerase to replicate ends and prevent shortening.

⚠️ Module 4: Mutation and DNA Repair

Review the types of errors that can occur in the genetic code and the cellular mechanisms designed to fix them. Click to expand each category for specific mechanisms and consequences.

By Base Change:
  • Transitions: Purine to Purine (A↔G) or Pyrimidine to Pyrimidine (C↔T).
  • Transversions: Purine to Pyrimidine or vice versa.
Functional Consequence:
  • Silent Changes codon, same amino acid.
  • Missense Changes amino acid (e.g., sickle cell).
  • Nonsense Creates premature stop codon.

Frameshift Mutations: Insertions or deletions (indels) of base pairs not in multiples of 3. This alters the entire downstream reading frame, usually resulting in a non-functional protein.

Spontaneous vs. Induced: Spontaneous mutations arise from natural processes like tautomeric shifts or replication errors. Induced mutations are caused by mutagens (UV light, X-rays, intercalating agents).

Single-Strand Repair
  • Mismatch Repair (MMR): Fixes replication errors immediately. Detects daughter strand via lack of methylation (in prokaryotes).
  • Base Excision Repair (BER): Removes small, non-helix-distorting lesions using DNA glycosylases.
  • Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER): Repairs bulky lesions (e.g., UV thymine dimers).
Double-Strand Break Repair
  • Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ): Error-prone, glues ends back together without a template.
  • Homologous Recombination (HR): Error-free, uses sister chromatid as a template for precise repair.

🌐 Module 5: Population & Quantitative Genetics

Shift focus from individuals to populations. The interactive Hardy-Weinberg simulator below demonstrates how allele frequencies ($p$ and $q$) mathematically determine the distribution of genotypes in a population assumed to be in equilibrium.

Hardy-Weinberg Simulator

Assumes no mutation, random mating, infinite size, no gene flow, and no selection.

p² + 2pq + q² = 1

Evolutionary Forces

Forces that violate H-W equilibrium and cause evolution:

  • Genetic Drift: Random fluctuations (Bottleneck, Founder effect). Strongest in small populations.
  • Gene Flow: Migration of alleles between populations.
  • Natural Selection: Differential survival (Directional, Stabilizing, Disruptive).

Quantitative Genetics

Analyzes complex, continuous traits.

Phenotypic Variance: VP = VG + VE + VG×E
Heritability:
  • Broad-sense (H²): VG / VP
  • Narrow-sense (h²): VA / VP (Additive only; determines response to selection).
Response to Selection: R = h² × S

🧪 Module 6: Genomics & Advanced Biotech

The final module covers modern applications of genetic knowledge. This includes manipulating DNA in the lab, amplifying sequences, sequencing entire genomes, and cutting-edge gene-editing technologies.

Recombinant DNA

Joining DNA from different sources. Utilizes restriction enzymes (endonucleases) to cut, and DNA ligase to paste. Plasmids are often used as vectors.

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

Amplifies specific DNA sequences. Steps:

  1. Denaturation (95°C)
  2. Annealing (50-65°C)
  3. Extension (72°C using Taq)

Sequencing

  • Sanger: Uses chain-terminating dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs).
  • NGS (Next-Gen): High-throughput, massively parallel sequencing (e.g., Illumina).

CRISPR-Cas9

Revolutionary gene-editing tool derived from bacterial immune systems. Uses a guide RNA (gRNA) to direct the Cas9 nuclease to a specific genomic location to induce a double-strand break. This break is then repaired by the cell to either knock out or precisely knock in genes.

The "Omics"

  • Genomics: Whole genomes.
  • Transcriptomics: All RNA transcripts (RNA-Seq, Microarrays).
  • Proteomics: All expressed proteins.
